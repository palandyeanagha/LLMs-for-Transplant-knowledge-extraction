Abstract

Introduction
The kidney plays a leading role in maintaining calcium
and phosphorus homeostasis in collaboration with other orï¿¾gans, i.e., the parathyroid gland, intestines, and bones. It is
not only the target organ for various hormones, such as
parathyroid hormone (PTH), but also the principal site for
the production of calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D).
Thus, along with the progression of chronic kidney disease
(CKD), various abnormalities of mineral and bone metaboï¿¾lism develop, which can result in significant consequences
(1). Traditionally, such disorders have been considered with
regard to the bone lesion itself, but it has become increasï¿¾ingly evident that mineral and bone disorders have a critical
role in the pathogenesis of extraskeletal calcification includï¿¾ing the vasculature, thereby resulting in cardiovascular comï¿¾plications and mortality. Thus, in place of the term â€˜renal
osteodystrophy (ROD)â€™, a new term, â€˜CKD-mineral and
bone disorders (CKD-MBD)â€™ has recently been proposed to
describe this broad clinical syndrome that develops as a sysï¿¾temic disorder (Fig. 1) (2). Following this paradigm shift,
the Japanese Society for Dialysis Therapy (JSDT) released
the Guidelines for the Management of Secondary Hyperï¿¾parathyroidism in Chronic Dialysis Patients, which prioriï¿¾tized on improvement in survival, but not in bone abnorï¿¾malities (3, 4). Here, we review the current strategies for the
treatment of CKD-MBD.

Control of Hyperphosphatemia
Among the risk factors related to vascular calcification,
hyperphosphatemia is evidently the major contributing facï¿¾tor. It is well known that vascular calcification is not a simï¿¾ple process of passive mineralization, but also includes sevï¿¾eral mechanisms similar to bone formation that are exempliï¿¾fied by upregulation of osteochondrogenic differentiation
markers (10-12). Phosphate retention also stimulates PTH
secretion and induces parathyroid hyperplasia either directly
or indirectly (13, 14). In addition, higher serum phosphorus
and calcium-phosphorus product levels are associated with
the progression of CKD (15). Thus, there is every reason to
control the serum phosphorus levels in patients with CKD.
Dietary phosphorus restriction and conventional dialysis
alone are usually ineffective in controlling hyperphosï¿¾phatemia, hence, most dialysis patients require phosphorus
binders. Aluminum-based binders were once used extenï¿¾sively, but were abandoned when aluminum was found to
contribute to anemia, myopathies, dementia, and lowï¿¾turnover bone disease (16). Since then, calcium-based bindï¿¾ers have predominantly been in use, but they have been
linked to arterial calcification observed in hemodialysis paï¿¾tients (17). Recently, non-calcium and non-aluminum-based
binders such as sevelamer hydrochloride have been introï¿¾duced in clinical practice in Japan. Several studies have sugï¿¾gested that excessive calcium intake may worsen vascular
calcification (17), and that the progression of coronary vasï¿¾cular calcification was slower in patients on sevelamer, than
in those on calcium-based phosphorus binders (18, 19);
therefore, the K/DOQI guidelines restricted the total dose of
calcium carbonate to 3,750 mg/day, and recommended the
use of sevelamer, especially for patients with hypercalcemia
(5). In the JSDT guidelines, the total dose of calcium carï¿¾bonate was restricted to 3,000 mg/day, in consideration of
the body size of Japanese patients (3, 4).
Despite the possible beneficial effects of sevelamer on the
progression of vascular calcification, it is unknown whether
the choice of a phosphorus binder is likely to improve the
survival outcome of dialysis patients. It has recently been
shown that the use of calcium-based binders in an incident
dialysis population was associated with a significantly
higher mortality rate when compared to sevelamer (20). By
contrast, the Dialysis Clinical Outcomes Revisited study
failed to demonstrate a significant reduction in mortality in
patients treated with sevelamer compared to those receiving
calcium-based phosphorus binders, except for individuals
over 65 years of age (21). Therefore, the use of sevelamer
should be considered on a case-by-case basis, until further
well-designed studies clearly show its superiority over the
calcium-based phosphorus binder. Given that many Japanese
patients suffer from adverse events such as severe constipaï¿¾tion, combination treatment with sevelamer and calcium carï¿¾bonate may be effective and tolerable (22).
A new salt form of sevelamer carbonate has recently been
developed and is in the experimental stage (23). This new
agent seems to be especially useful in patients with CKD
who are not yet receiving hemodialysis, because it does not
induce hyperchloremic acidosis. It is also promising for curï¿¾rent hemodialysis patients, as it eliminates the need for

Management of Secondary
Hyperparathyroidism
Treatment of secondary hyperparathyroidism is the corï¿¾nerstone in the management of CKD-MBD. It is well estabï¿¾lished that phosphorus retention and calcitriol deficiency
play critical roles in the pathogenesis of secondary hyperï¿¾parathyroidism (29). Recent clinical studies revealed that fiï¿¾broblast growth factor 23, a newly identified phosphaturic
factor, is progressively elevated to prevent hyperphosï¿¾phatemia in CKD, at the expense of low calcitriol and hyï¿¾perparathyroidism (30, 31). Thus, rigorous control of serum
phosphorus levels and administration of calcitriol or its anaï¿¾logs are the mainstream treatment for secondary hyperparaï¿¾thyroidism (5).
Before initiating treatment for secondary hyperparathyï¿¾roidism, clinicians should recall the JSDT guidelines, which
state that normalization of serum phosphorus and calcium
concentrations is a prerequisite for PTH control (3, 4). Calï¿¾citriol has been shown to suppress PTH secretion effectively
and inhibit cell proliferation in parathyroid hyperplasia (32,
33), but this treatment may increase the risk for hypercalceï¿¾mia and hyperphosphatemia, resulting in withdrawal or a reï¿¾duction in the dose of calcitriol. To overcome these probï¿¾lems, new calcitriol analogs with less calcemic action than
calcitriol, such as paricalcitol (34), doxercalciferol (35),
maxacalcitol (36), and falecalcitriol (37) have been develï¿¾oped. Among these, maxacalcitol and falecalcitriol are now
commercially available in Japan. However, even maxacalciï¿¾tol treatment readily induces hypercalcemia in patients with
advanced hyperparathyroidism, and parathyroid interventions
such as parathyroidectomy are often required (38). Thus,
identifying a patient with refractory hyperparathyroidism is
a major problem.
Recently, pathophysiological aspects of parathyroid hyperï¿¾plasia have been extensively elucidated (39, 40). In the inï¿¾itial stage of CKD, several mitogenic stimuli, such as phosï¿¾phorus load and reduced production of calcitriol, induce hyï¿¾pertrophy and proliferation of parathyroid cells (diffuse hyï¿¾perplasia). Some cells in the parathyroid with diffuse hyperï¿¾plasia, especially those exhibiting reduction of vitamin D reï¿¾ceptor (VDR) and calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR), prolifï¿¾erate vigorously and become encapsulated (nodular hyperï¿¾plasia). It has been well recognized that cells in nodular hyï¿¾perplasia have reduced numbers of VDR and CaSR, thus
usually refractory to medical therapies (41, 42). Developï¿¾ment of nodular hyperplasia has been most efficiently diagï¿¾nosed by ultrasonography, and 0.5 cm3 or 1 cm in diameter
has been considered to be the critical size (43). Hence, diï¿¾alysis patients with severe hyperparathyroidism and such
large parathyroid gland(s) should undergo parathyroid interï¿¾vention if they do not respond to a short course of medical
intervention (Fig. 2) (44).
Parathyroid intervention
Parathyroid intervention, i.e., surgical parathyroidectomy
and direct injection therapy, should be indicated for refracï¿¾tory hyperparathyroidism associated with nodular hyperplaï¿¾sia. There are three surgical approaches: total parathyroidecï¿¾tomy with or without autotransplantation, subtotal parathyï¿¾roidectomy, and minimally invasive surgery (45, 46). In genï¿¾eral, total parathyroidectomy with forearm autograft is preï¿¾ferred for secondary hyperparathyroidism, especially in paï¿¾tients who require long-term hemodialysis, because a recurï¿¾rent, enlarged autograft can easily be removed from the
forearm (45). Minimally invasive surgery may be effective
and less invasive in patients with one solitary radioactive
nodule (46). Direct injection therapies, such as percutaneous
ethanol injection therapy (PEIT), percutaneous calcitriol inï¿¾jection therapy (PCIT), and percutaneous maxacalcitol injecï¿¾tion therapy (PMIT) are also effective treatments for refracï¿¾tory hyperparathyroidism (47-50). The main difference beï¿¾tween parathyroidectomy and direct injection therapy is the
selection of the target glands. In total parathyroidectomy
with autograft, all glands are surgically removed and fragï¿¾ments from the smallest gland are transplanted in the foreï¿¾arm. In direct injection therapy, an enlarged gland with
nodular hyperplasia is selectively infiltrated by ethanol or
calcitriol (analogs), and other glands with diffuse hyperplaï¿¾sia are treated with medical therapy. The appropriate dose of
calcitriol or its analogs must be used soon after direct injecï¿¾tion therapy (51). In a recent clinical study, patients with
one hyperplastic gland responded well to PEIT with respect
to efficacy rate, remission period, and risk of relapse (52).
As recommended by the JSDT guidelines, a patient with
one enlarged gland is the best indication for PEIT (3, 4).
The selection of the optimal method for parathyroid interï¿¾vention should be determined by the number and location of
enlarged glands, as well as the presence of ectopic glands.

Calcimimetic agents
Calcimimetic agents, such as cinacalcet hydrochloride,
have been developed for the control of hyperparathyroidism
in patients with CKD. These agents enhance the sensitivity
of the parathyroid calcium-sensing receptors, thereby reducï¿¾ing levels of PTH, serum calcium and phosphorus, and
calcium-phosphorus product (53). Cinacalcet therapy is also
favorable to meet the practice guidelines. The combined data
from three large randomized, placebo-controlled studies
showed that treatment with cinacalcet in combination with
conventional therapy, led to better achievement of the K/
DOQI recommended target ranges (54). In Japan, a recent
clinical study showed the efficacy and safety of cinacalcet
therapy, even in patients with a longer average dialysis vinï¿¾tage (55). A post hoc analysis of the combined data from
four similarly designed clinical trials showed that cinacalcet
treatment significantly decreased the risks of parathyroidecï¿¾tomy, fracture, and cardiovascular hospitalization (56). It reï¿¾mains, however, to be elucidated whether or not calcimimetï¿¾ics can effectively control hyperparathyroidism in patients
with nodular hyperplasia (57). Moreover, the economic imï¿¾pact of these expensive agents should be considered, espeï¿¾cially in comparison with parathyroidectomy (58). Further
investigation is required to elucidate these issues and estabï¿¾lish the clinical significance of cinacalcet therapy in patients
with CKD.
Possible Beneficial Effect of Vitamin D
Therapy on Survival
Several clinical data uncovered CKD as a risk factor for
vitamin D deficiency and current recommendations from K/
DOQI emphasize the need to correct this deficiency by adï¿¾ministration of a vitamin D preparation such as ergocalcifï¿¾erol. As 1-hydroxylase activity is impaired in CKD, actiï¿¾vated vitamin D offers theoretical advantages and has been
prescribed to dialysis patients. Recently, a retrospective
analysis of hemodialysis patients reported that the mortality
was lower among those treated with paricalcitol, a selective
activator of VDR, compared to calcitriol (59). Since then,
several investigators have speculated that calcitriol or its
analogs may reduce cardiovascular morbidity and mortality
in patients with CKD, especially dialysis patients. The
mechanism of such an effect is not known, but one hypotheï¿¾sis suggests that calcitriol may have non-classical direct efï¿¾fects on the cardiovascular system (60). However, the effect
of correction of calcitriol deficiency on cardiovascular morï¿¾tality is not evident due to the retrospective nature of previï¿¾ous studies and the potential for confounding by unknown
factors. Indeed, a recent systematic review and meta-analysis
revealed that the evidence does not support the widespread
use of calcitriol (analogs) in treatment of CKD (61). By
contrast, a recent prospective analysis of case-control data
indicated that incident dialysis patients who received paricalï¿¾citol had a significant survival benefit during the first 90
days of dialysis, compared with those who did not receive
calcitriol or its analogs (62). Further detailed studies are reï¿¾quired to validate the beneficial effect of selective VDR actiï¿¾vators in CKD patients.
Conclusions
Disturbance in mineral and bone metabolism is one of the
most important causes of increased cardiovascular mortality
in CKD. According to recent clinical guidelines, including
those released by the JSDT, the control of parathyroid funcï¿¾tion in CKD patients should be achieved without the risk of
