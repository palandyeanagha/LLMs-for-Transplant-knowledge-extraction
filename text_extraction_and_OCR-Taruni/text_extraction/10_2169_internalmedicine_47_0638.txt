Introduction
A pyogenic liver abscess is a potentially life-threatening
disease. Epidemiology, management, mortality rate have all
undergone significant changes over the past several decades
(1, 2). Among the intraabdominal processes leading to abï¿¾scess formation, ruptured appendicitis has been the most
common route for formation of liver abscess. However, biliï¿¾ary tract pathology is now the most common cause of pyoï¿¾genic liver abscesses, accounting for 43 to 60 percent of
cases (1, 2). Malignant biliary obstruction has become a
more frequent cause in the last several decades. By contrast,
hematogenous seeding is not the usual pathway for developï¿¾ment of a liver abscess. We present an immunocompetent
patient with an anaerobic pyogenic liver abscess caused by
Fusobacterium nucleatum, a bacterium commonly found in
the oral cavity.

Discussion

spread of infection was the most common route for the deï¿¾velopment of liver abscesses. Currently, an associated disï¿¾ease of the biliary tract is the most often seen etiology (1,
2). However, hematogenous spread of infection is not comï¿¾mon, especially that originating from the oral cavity. In the
present case, because the patient refused, we could not perï¿¾form percutaneous drainage of the liver abscess. Diagnosis
was done based on ultrasonography, CT, and clinical sympï¿¾toms, and the result was a complete recovery from just antiï¿¾biotic treatment. Because we did not find the focus of sepsis

Fusobacterium nucleatum, an anaerobic Gram-negative
non-sporeforming oral bacterium found in the normal flora
of the mouth, has a role in periodontal disease (3). It proï¿¾duces tissue irritants such as butyric acid, proteases and cyï¿¾tokines. It has strong adhesive properties because of the
presence of lectins, and these outer-membrane proteins meï¿¾diate adhesion to epithelia and tooth surfaces, and coagglutiï¿¾nation with other suspected pathogens (3). Fusobacterium
species are known to be a cause of Lemierreâ€™s syndrome (4)
and aspiration pneumonia. However, an association between
Fusobacterium species and pyogenic liver abscesses has not
been confirmed. Our review of the literature revealed only
12 cases of liver abscess due to Fusobacterium nucleatum
reported (Table 1) (5-15). In eight, the authors described
whether or not the patient was immunocompetent. Five of
the 8 (63%), patients were immunocompromised hosts with
a history of transplantation, malignancy, long term steroid
use, or HIV infection. Only in 3 cases (37%), was the paï¿¾tient immunocompetent with no history of surgery. Two of
those patients (67%) had dental diseases (9), while the other
had a tonsil infection that was thought to be the entry point
for Fusobacterium nucleatum in the oral cavity (12).
Immunodeficient patients are likely to develop opportunisï¿¾tic or superimposed infection. Liver transplantation, diabeï¿¾tes, and a history of malignancy are risk factors for pyoï¿¾genic liver abscess (16). However, poor oral hygiene might
also be an independent risk factor of the disease. Usually,
hematogenous spread of bacteria occurs after interventional
treatment of dental disease. The present case had no history
of dental treatment;however, under conditions of very poor
oral hygiene with severe periodontal disease, hematogenous
spread of common oral bacteria may occur. We suggest that
maintenance of adequate oral hygiene, especially profesï¿¾sional mechanical tooth cleaning (PMTC) and appropriate
self care (17) are important not only for dental, but also for
systemic diseases.
Although the association of alcohol and amoebic liver abï¿¾scess has been reported (18), the association between alcoï¿¾hol and pyogenic liver abscess has not been fully clarified.
The present case had a history of alcohol intake, however,
laboratory data and imaging studies did not show chronic
liver injury.
In conclusion, we presented an unusual case of a pyoï¿¾genic liver abscess due to Fusobacterium nucleatum in an
immunocompetent host. In our review of the literature, most
cases of pyogenic liver abscesses were immunodeficient
states;however, poor oral hygiene appears to be an indeï¿¾pendent and under-recognized risk factor for this disease.
Appropriate oral care is clearly important not only for denï¿¾tal, but also for systemic diseases.
