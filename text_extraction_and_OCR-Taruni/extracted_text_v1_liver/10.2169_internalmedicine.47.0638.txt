Pyogenic Liver Abscess Related to Dental Disease
in an Immunocompetent Host
Takashi Kajiya 1,3, Takeshi Uemura 1, Mami Kajiya 2, Hikaru Kaname 2, Ryuki Hirano1,
Nobuhiro Uemura 1 and Chuwa Tei 3
Abstract
A 59-year-old man with poor oral hygiene presented to our hospital because of fever and chills. Abdominal ultrasonography and enhanced computed tomography (CT) revealed a liver abscess. The patient had no
history of immunodeficiency and we confirmed the patient had no immunologic abnormalities. Blood culture
revealed Fusobacterium nucleatum, a bacterium commonly found in the oral cavity. Even if a patient is immunocompetent, poor oral hygiene might be an independent risk factor for a pyogenic liver abscess. Professional mechanical tooth cleaning (PMTC) and appropriate self-care are recommended as a prophylaxis
against not only dental, but also systemic diseases.
Key words: liver abscess, fusobacterium nucleatum, periodontal disease, professional mechanical tooth cleaning

Introduction
A pyogenic liver abscess is a potentially life-threatening
disease. Epidemiology, management, mortality rate have all
undergone significant changes over the past several decades
(1, 2). Among the intraabdominal processes leading to abscess formation, ruptured appendicitis has been the most
common route for formation of liver abscess. However, biliary tract pathology is now the most common cause of pyogenic liver abscesses, accounting for 43 to 60 percent of
cases (1, 2). Malignant biliary obstruction has become a
more frequent cause in the last several decades. By contrast,
hematogenous seeding is not the usual pathway for development of a liver abscess. We present an immunocompetent
patient with an anaerobic pyogenic liver abscess caused by
Fusobacterium nucleatum, a bacterium commonly found in
the oral cavity.

Discussion
In the past, ruptured appendicitis and the subsequent
spread of infection was the most common route for the development of liver abscesses. Currently, an associated disease of the biliary tract is the most often seen etiology (1,
2). However, hematogenous spread of infection is not common, especially that originating from the oral cavity. In the
present case, because the patient refused, we could not perform percutaneous drainage of the liver abscess. Diagnosis
was done based on ultrasonography, CT, and clinical symptoms, and the result was a complete recovery from just antibiotic treatment. Because we did not find the focus of sepsis
on admission, we selected imipenem/cilastatin as an empiric

and are subsequently spread through the arterial circulation.
This would lead to spread through the hepatic artery. We
speculate that in the present case abscess formation was
found in early stage in sepsis. If the beginning of antibiotic
treatments was delayed, abscess formation would spread to
systemic organs.
Fusobacterium nucleatum, an anaerobic Gram-negative
non-sporeforming oral bacterium found in the normal flora
of the mouth, has a role in periodontal disease (3). It produces tissue irritants such as butyric acid, proteases and cytokines. It has strong adhesive properties because of the
presence of lectins, and these outer-membrane proteins mediate adhesion to epithelia and tooth surfaces, and coagglutination with other suspected pathogens (3). Fusobacterium
species are known to be a cause of Lemierre‚Äôs syndrome (4)
and aspiration pneumonia. However, an association between
Fusobacterium species and pyogenic liver abscesses has not
been confirmed. Our review of the literature revealed only
12 cases of liver abscess due to Fusobacterium nucleatum
reported (Table 1) (5-15). In eight, the authors described
whether or not the patient was immunocompetent. Five of
the 8 (63%), patients were immunocompromised hosts with
a history of transplantation, malignancy, long term steroid
use, or HIV infection. Only in 3 cases (37%), was the patient immunocompetent with no history of surgery. Two of
those patients (67%) had dental diseases (9), while the other
had a tonsil infection that was thought to be the entry point
for Fusobacterium nucleatum in the oral cavity (12).
Immunodeficient patients are likely to develop opportunistic or superimposed infection. Liver transplantation, diabetes, and a history of malignancy are risk factors for pyogenic liver abscess (16). However, poor oral hygiene might
also be an independent risk factor of the disease. Usually,
hematogenous spread of bacteria occurs after interventional
treatment of dental disease. The present case had no history
of dental treatment;however, under conditions of very poor
oral hygiene with severe periodontal disease, hematogenous
spread of common oral bacteria may occur. We suggest that
maintenance of adequate oral hygiene, especially professional mechanical tooth cleaning (PMTC) and appropriate
self care (17) are important not only for dental, but also for
systemic diseases.
Although the association of alcohol and amoebic liver abscess has been reported (18), the association between alcohol and pyogenic liver abscess has not been fully clarified.
The present case had a history of alcohol intake, however,
laboratory data and imaging studies did not show chronic
liver injury.
In conclusion, we presented an unusual case of a pyogenic liver abscess due to Fusobacterium nucleatum in an
immunocompetent host. In our review of the literature, most
cases of pyogenic liver abscesses were immunodeficient
states;however, poor oral hygiene appears to be an independent and under-recognized risk factor for this disease.
Appropriate oral care is clearly important not only for dental, but also for systemic diseases.
